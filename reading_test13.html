FROZEN LAND

List of Headings:

D. Race to the Pole

F. Where is the end of the Earth?

H. Why is it so cold?

1. Unlike most places in the world, Antarctica does not belong to any single country. Instead, it is governed by an international treaty signed by multiple nations. The Antarctic Treaty, established in 1959, ensures that the continent is used only for peaceful purposes, mainly scientific research, and prohibits military activity.

2. The first documented landing on Antarctica took place in 1821 by a group of seal hunters. However, the first real exploration of the continent happened much later. Scientists and adventurers faced extreme conditions to set foot on this frozen land, collecting valuable information about the climate and wildlife.

3. Antarctica is often called “the end of the Earth” because of its remote location. It is the southernmost continent, and its vast icy landscapes make it feel like another planet. Despite its isolation, scientists from around the world travel there to study its unique environment and the effects of climate change.

4. When people imagine Antarctica, they usually think of an endless white desert. However, underneath the thick ice, there are hidden mountains, valleys, and even lakes. Scientists have used radar and satellite images to study the land beneath the ice, discovering features such as Lake Vostok, a massive subglacial lake buried under more than 3,700 meters of ice.

5. In the early 20th century, explorers from different nations competed to be the first to reach the South Pole. The most famous rivalry was between Roald Amundsen from Norway and Robert Falcon Scott from the United Kingdom. Amundsen's team arrived first in December 1911, while Scott's team arrived a month later but tragically did not survive the return journey.

6. Walking through deep snow and ice can be extremely difficult. However, in Antarctica, sled dogs and specially designed vehicles have made movement easier. Scientists also use kites and wind-powered sleds to travel long distances with less effort, using the strong Antarctic winds to their advantage.

7. Antarctica is one of the coldest places on Earth. The extreme temperatures are caused by the continent’s location at the South Pole, where the Sun's rays hit at a low angle, providing little warmth. In addition, the high altitude and strong winds make the climate even harsher, making it one of the most uninhabitable regions in the world.
