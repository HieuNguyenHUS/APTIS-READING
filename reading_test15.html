Read the passage quickly. Choose a heading for each numbered paragraph (1–7) from the drop-down box. There is one more heading than you need.

Arrival four days working

List of Headings:

B. Alternative solutions worth considering
C. An unfair plan for some people
D. Benefits for employees
E. Difficult to change old habits
F. Impact on the environment (Extra heading)
G. Undesirable financial consequences
H. Unforeseen challenges for employees

1. The growing discussion around a four-day working week suggests that the traditional five-day schedule is losing its appeal. Many companies still rely on outdated, industrial-age patterns, believing long hours guarantee higher performance. However, today's employees are no longer satisfied with this rigid approach. They increasingly value balance, flexibility, and personal wellbeing over mere presence at the office. In many cases, long working hours bring stress and exhaustion rather than productivity. As a result, the old system is slowly becoming less relevant to modern life.

2. A shorter working week offers employees clear and meaningful advantages. The standard five-day routine leaves little time for family, health, or leisure, which often leads to fatigue. For women in particular, the challenge is greater, since many carry responsibility for both work and household duties. An extra day off allows people to recharge and focus on personal needs, making them happier and more balanced. This balance can then increase motivation and commitment in the workplace. In the long term, such benefits may raise overall productivity.

3. Many business owners argue that fewer working hours will inevitably reduce profit. Evidence from real cases shows that these concerns cannot be ignored. In the UK, one company trialled the policy and reported a 30% drop in output. Similarly, in one city, the change caused losses of around 30 million dollars in just one month. These examples make many employers reluctant to introduce the system on a wide scale.

4. The shorter week may also create unexpected difficulties for employees. Although some firms report that work is still completed on time, this often comes at a cost. Compressing tasks into fewer hours can increase mistakes and raise stress levels. Workers sometimes take projects home, which defeats the purpose of having more free time. Over time, this added pressure can reduce rather than improve wellbeing. Therefore, the model may not be as sustainable as it first appears.

5. The pursuit of the southernmost point in the early 20th century captivated many. Adventurers such as Robert Falcon Scott and Roald Amundsen undertook perilous journeys to achieve this milestone. Amundsen's successful expedition in 1911 marked a significant achievement in exploration, while Scott's tragic story underscored the risks associated with such endeavors. These tales of courage and ambition continue to inspire modern explorers, highlighting the fascination that this harsh terrain holds.

6. While a shorter working week may seem attractive, it does not benefit everyone equally. Employees in education, healthcare, and emergency services cannot simply reduce their hours without harming those who depend on them. Similarly, small businesses may lack the resources to reorganize schedules or hire extra staff. As a result, the policy risks creating inequality: office workers may enjoy more free time, while others are forced to continue with long and exhausting shifts. If fairness is not addressed, the four-day week could deepen divisions between different sectors of society.

7. Instead of focusing only on reducing the number of working days, other reforms may bring broader and fairer results. Flexible hours, remote work opportunities, or job-sharing can give employees greater control over their routines without cutting productivity. Companies can also invest in better training, digital tools, and mental health support to improve efficiency and wellbeing. By adopting a mix of strategies, employers may create healthier workplaces that serve both business needs and individual lives. In this way, balance and satisfaction can be achieved without the risks tied to a strict four-day model.
